Special Investigation: Sin Taxes & Regressive Burden

The Tax That Claims to Punish Vice
Punishes the Poor Instead

For six decades, the United States has taxed cigarettes, alcohol, and lottery tickets as instruments of public health. What that framing obscures is that the burden falls heaviest on the households least able to bear it, and that the same governments discouraging vice depend on its continuation for revenue.

In 1964, the Surgeon General's report gave Congress the rationale to raise cigarette excise taxes on public-health grounds. That same year, New Hampshire authorized the nation's first modern state lottery, inaugurating a parallel stream of government vice revenue that would, over six decades, grow to exceed federal tobacco tax receipts by a factor of more than two. The contradiction embedded in that pairing has compounded ever since.

A price-based deterrent works only if the household subject to it can afford to respond. A household with financial flexibility can reduce consumption when cigarette prices rise; one without that flexibility simply pays more. The tax achieves its stated objective most reliably among those already best positioned to achieve it on their own.

"For a tax to change behavior, the household subject to it must possess the capacity to alter the behavior in question. Where that capacity is absent, the price signal does not discourage consumption; it merely increases its cost."

BLS Consumer Expenditure Survey Analysis, 2022

BLS Consumer Expenditure Survey data shows that bottom-quintile households devote 14.3 percent of annual income to sin-taxed products, while top-quintile households devote approximately 1.0 percent. The nominal tax rate is identical across both; the burden is not. Exhibit A reconstructs the same purchase record across five income levels to make that disparity concrete.

Act I · 1964–1983 The Framing: When Deterrence Was the Official Story

The federal excise tax on tobacco was initially a revenue instrument, its public-health justification arriving after the Surgeon General's 1964 report gave policymakers a more convenient rationale. Each subsequent rate increase was presented as health stewardship rather than fiscal extraction, but the arithmetic did not change. By 2023, a pack of cigarettes carried a combined excise burden of approximately 44 percent of retail price, identical for every purchaser regardless of income.

For a household earning $15,000 annually, a regular cigarette habit represents a meaningful fraction of disposable income. For one earning $250,000, the same expenditure is negligible. The 1964 report asked whether smoking caused disease; the tax policy built on its foundation has never formally asked whether that policy falls equitably across the population it claims to protect.

Same Store. Different Bill.

A pack of cigarettes is priced identically in a low-income neighborhood and an affluent one. The excise tax embedded in that price is the same in both cases. What differs is the share of annual income the purchase represents. Treasury Department analyses from the 1970s identified this regressive character explicitly; the public-health framing did not change the arithmetic, only the political difficulty of challenging it.

"A flat excise tax is, by the arithmetic of proportionality, a regressive burden, its claim on household income rising precisely as the household's capacity to bear that claim declines."

Treasury Department distributional analysis, 1974
Act II · 1983–2005 The Divergence: The Tax Worked, Though Not for Everyone.

When Congress doubled the cigarette excise tax in 1983, smoking rates declined at the population level. Among counties in the top income quintile, 75 percent saw statistically significant reductions in smoking prevalence. Among counties in the bottom quintile, the figure was 14 percent. Where households lack the financial flexibility to respond to a price signal, the tax does not discourage the behavior; it merely increases its cost.

Act III · 2000s–Present The State as Dealer: Half a Billion in Ads, Zero Apology

State lotteries generated $25 billion in government revenues in 2019 on $87 billion in ticket sales, surpassing both federal estate and tobacco tax receipts. States spent an estimated $562 million advertising these products, more than most states allocate to smoking cessation and problem gambling treatment combined. A government that depends on lottery revenue has a structural interest in sustained participation, an interest that shapes where terminals are placed, how products are priced, and how aggressively they are promoted.

"A government that depends on lottery revenue to fund public services has a structural interest in ensuring that its citizens continue to purchase lottery tickets — a fact that fundamentally complicates the public-health framing of its taxation policy."

The Receipt the Government Never Shows You

The sin tax system presents itself as a deterrent to harmful behavior. What the data show is that its behavioral effects are concentrated among households with the financial capacity to respond to price signals, while the burden falls most heavily on those without it. The same governments raising cigarette taxes are simultaneously advertising state lotteries whose revenue model depends on continued participation by the same population.
