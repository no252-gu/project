Special Investigation — Sin Taxes & Regressive Burden

The Tax That Claims to Punish Vice
Punishes the Poor Instead

For more than six decades, the United States government has justified excise taxes on tobacco, alcohol, and gambling as instruments of public health — mechanisms through which the price of harmful behavior is raised until the behavior becomes financially untenable. What that framing has consistently obscured, however, is the degree to which the deterrent effect of a price signal depends upon the financial capacity of the household receiving it, and the extent to which states have come to depend on the very consumption they claim to discourage.

In 1964, the United States Surgeon General released the first conclusive federal statement on the health consequences of cigarette smoking, providing Congress with the scientific foundation it required to justify escalating excise taxes on tobacco products as instruments of public health policy. That same year, and largely without irony, the state of New Hampshire authorized the nation's first modern state lottery — inaugurating a second stream of government-managed vice revenue that would, over the following six decades, grow to exceed the federal tobacco tax yield by a factor of more than two. These two policy decisions, arrived at within the same calendar year and framed through entirely different justifications, represent the foundational contradiction at the center of American sin taxation: a system that presents itself as a mechanism for discouraging harmful behavior while simultaneously constructing and expanding a financial dependency on that behavior's continuation.

The distributional consequences of this system have been extensively documented in academic literature, though considerably less so in the public framing of these policies. What the evidence reveals is that the effectiveness of a price-based deterrent — the mechanism through which excise taxes are meant to reduce consumption — is not uniform across the income distribution; rather, it is itself a function of income. A household that possesses the financial flexibility to substitute, to reduce consumption, or to seek cessation support can respond to a higher cigarette price by doing so. A household that lacks that flexibility cannot, and instead simply pays more for an unchanged pattern of behavior, such that the tax accomplishes its stated objective most reliably among those who were already best positioned to achieve it independently.

"For a tax to change behavior, the household subject to it must possess the capacity to alter the behavior in question. Where that capacity is absent, the price signal does not discourage consumption — it merely increases its cost."

— BLS Consumer Expenditure Survey Analysis, 2022

Bureau of Labor Statistics Consumer Expenditure Survey data indicates that a household in the bottom income quintile devotes, on average, 14.3 percent of its annual income to products subject to sin taxes — cigarettes, alcohol, and lottery tickets combined — while a household in the top quintile allocates approximately 1.0 percent of its income to the same category. The nominal tax rate applied to each transaction is identical across both households; the economic burden that those transactions represent is not. Exhibit A, reproduced below, reconstructs the same monthly purchase record across five income levels, demonstrating precisely how a flat excise tax translates into a sharply regressive burden when measured as a proportion of household income rather than as a nominal dollar amount.

The modern American lottery was not born from gambling culture; it was born from fiscal desperation. Facing a persistent budget shortfall, New Hampshire authorized a sweepstakes in the same year the Surgeon General declared smoking a public health emergency. That pairing was not ironic by design. It became ironic by consequence — and that consequence has compounded steadily for six decades.

Act I · 1964–1983 The Framing: When Deterrence Was the Official Story

The federal excise tax on tobacco products was initially codified as a revenue instrument, its public-health justification arriving largely after the fact — once the Surgeon General's 1964 report provided policymakers with a more compelling rationale for a policy that had already been in place for more than a decade. This retroactive reframing proved both durable and politically convenient, allowing each successive increase in the excise rate to be presented as an act of health stewardship rather than fiscal extraction. The arithmetic of the policy, however, did not change with its framing, and the distributional consequences that Treasury Department analyses had identified as early as the 1970s persisted, quietly and steadily, beneath the public-health language that surrounded them.

By 2023, a pack of cigarettes carried a combined state and federal excise burden representing approximately 44 percent of its retail price before any additional sales tax. This burden is identical for every purchaser, regardless of income, education, or financial circumstance. For a household earning $15,000 annually — the approximate median of households in the bottom income quintile, according to BLS Consumer Expenditure Survey data — the monthly expenditure associated with a regular cigarette habit represents a meaningful fraction of disposable income. For a household earning $250,000, the same expenditure is financially negligible. The Surgeon General's 1964 report asked whether smoking caused disease; the tax policy constructed on its foundation has never formally asked whether the financial mechanism designed to address that disease falls equitably across the population it was designed to protect.

Same Store. Different Bill.

The purchase record reconstructed in Exhibit A does not misrepresent the retail environment: a pack of cigarettes is priced identically in a lower-income neighborhood and an affluent one, and the excise tax embedded in that price is the same in both cases. What differs is not the price on the shelf but the proportion of a household's annual income required to sustain the same consumption pattern. A flat dollar excise tax is, by the arithmetic of proportionality, a regressive burden — its claim on household income rising precisely as the household's capacity to bear that claim declines.

This distributional property was not unknown to policymakers. Treasury Department analyses from the 1970s explicitly identified the regressive character of commodity excise taxes and categorized them, alongside state sales taxes, as placing a disproportionate financial burden on lower-income households. The adoption of a public-health framing for tobacco taxation did not alter this arithmetic; it altered the political environment in which that arithmetic was evaluated, rendering a structurally regressive policy substantially more difficult to challenge without appearing to endorse the behavior it was designed to address. This dynamic — in which a legitimate public-health objective provides cover for a distributional outcome that would otherwise be difficult to defend — has remained largely intact for six decades.

"A flat excise tax is, by the arithmetic of proportionality, a regressive burden — its claim on household income rising precisely as the household's capacity to bear that claim declines."

— Treasury Department distributional analysis, 1974
Act II · 1983–2005 The Divergence: The Tax Worked. Just Not for Everyone.

When Congress doubled the federal cigarette excise tax in 1983 — from eight cents to sixteen cents per pack, the first increase since 1951 — smoking rates did, in fact, decline. This outcome was cited at the time as evidence that price-based deterrence was functioning as intended, and the subsequent public health literature has consistently affirmed that higher cigarette prices are associated with reduced consumption at the population level. What that population-level framing obscures, however, is the distributional pattern beneath the aggregate — a pattern that becomes visible only when smoking rates are examined by income quintile rather than for the population as a whole.

Research examining county-level smoking trends in the years following the 1983 increase found that 75 percent of counties in the highest income quintile experienced statistically significant declines in smoking prevalence. Among counties in the lowest income quintile, the comparable figure was 14 percent. This disparity reflects a structural limitation of price-based deterrence that its advocates have rarely addressed directly: for a tax to change behavior, the household subject to it must possess the capacity to respond to the price signal it creates — through substitution, through access to cessation support, or through financial flexibility sufficient to absorb the cost of changing behavior. Where that capacity is absent, the price signal does not discourage the behavior; it merely increases its cost, extracting progressively more from a pattern of consumption that remains, in the relevant respects, unchanged.

Reading the Evidence

The smoking rate chart presented below traces two lines through the same historical period. Through the 1970s, smoking prevalence in top- and bottom-quintile counties declines at roughly comparable rates — a pattern consistent with the broad cultural and informational shifts that followed the 1964 Surgeon General's report, and that were not income-dependent in the same way that a price signal is. Following the 1983 federal tax increase, marked by the dotted vertical line on the chart, the two trajectories diverge sharply and permanently. Top-quintile counties show steep, sustained declines that have continued largely unabated through 2022; bottom-quintile counties show a flatter path of reduction that has leveled off well above zero and has effectively plateaued since the early 2000s.

The visual gap between those two lines is the distributional effect of the policy, made legible in aggregate data. It demonstrates that the 1983 tax increase produced significantly different behavioral outcomes depending on the economic resources available to the population subject to it — a finding that is difficult to reconcile with a public-health framing that treats price sensitivity as uniformly distributed across the income spectrum. The second chart below traces the federal excise rate from 1951 to 2023, illustrating each congressional increase and highlighting, in red, those years in which the rate exceeded 62 cents per pack — the threshold at which distributional research has found the regressive incidence to be most pronounced.

Act III · 2000s–Present The State as Dealer: Half a Billion in Ads, Zero Apology

The same legislative apparatus that has spent six decades raising cigarette excise taxes on public-health grounds has, during that same period, constructed and actively promoted a parallel system of state-sponsored gambling that generates substantial government revenue through precisely the type of consumption it nominally seeks to discourage. State lotteries generated approximately $25 billion in government revenues in 2019, on total ticket sales of $87 billion — representing an average expenditure of $679 per household. This figure surpassed both federal estate tax receipts and federal tobacco tax receipts in the same year, establishing the lottery as among the most significant revenue-generating instruments available to state governments. States spent an estimated $562 million advertising these products during the same period — a figure that exceeds, in most states, the combined budget allocated to smoking cessation and problem gambling treatment.

The fiscal logic underlying these expenditures is straightforward, if rarely stated plainly. A state government that depends on lottery revenue to fund public education, infrastructure, or general appropriations has a structural interest in sustained — and ideally growing — lottery participation. This interest does not diminish because it is inconvenient; it shapes, through ordinary institutional incentives, the decisions that state lottery agencies make about where to place terminals, how to design and price products, and how aggressively to advertise them. The result is a government that is, in the most literal sense, financially dependent on the continuation of the behavior it claims, through its taxation of tobacco and alcohol, to be actively working to discourage.

"A government that depends on lottery revenue to fund public services has a structural interest in ensuring that its citizens continue to purchase lottery tickets — a fact that fundamentally complicates the public-health framing of its taxation policy."

— Okweye, N. Vice Mart: The Hidden Tax. Georgetown DSAN 5200, 2026

Lottery Spending by Income

Bureau of Labor Statistics Consumer Expenditure Survey data indicates that households in the bottom income quintile allocate approximately 2.89 percent of their annual income to lottery ticket purchases, while households in the top quintile allocate approximately 0.27 percent — a tenfold disparity that closely mirrors the distributional pattern documented in the cigarette taxation literature. This inverse relationship between income and relative lottery spending cannot be explained by differences in jackpot values, which are identical across purchasers, nor is it readily attributable to uniform differences in risk preference. The more plausible explanation, supported by behavioral economics research, is that lottery participation among lower-income households is driven substantially by scarcity psychology — the perception that a small, regular expenditure on lottery tickets represents a credible pathway to financial mobility in an environment where conventional pathways are constrained.

The two charts below illustrate the distributional structure of lottery participation and revenue allocation. The first demonstrates the inverse relationship between income and relative spending burden, with each quintile's lottery expenditure expressed as a percentage of household income. The second breaks down the allocation of each dollar spent on a lottery ticket: approximately 28.7 cents flows directly to state government as revenue, 50.4 cents is returned to players as prizes, and the remainder covers retailer commissions and administrative costs. The structural implication is not complicated — the lottery is designed so that the state cannot lose — and the income data indicates that the households most likely to sustain that design are precisely the ones that can least afford the losses it produces.

The Receipt the Government Never Shows You

The sin tax system, examined as a complete policy architecture rather than as a collection of individual revenue instruments, presents a structural paradox that its public-health framing has consistently obscured. A set of taxes justified as deterrents to harmful behavior has operated, over the course of six decades, as a mechanism for extracting a disproportionate share of income from the households least capable of reducing the behavior being taxed — while the same government has simultaneously constructed, expanded, and actively marketed a parallel system of state-sponsored gambling whose revenue model depends on sustained participation by the same population.

This investigation does not argue that governments lack the authority to tax vice, nor does it dispute the well-documented finding that higher prices reduce aggregate tobacco consumption. The evidence on both points is sufficiently established to require no further rehearsal here. The consequential question is a different one: whether a tax that produces its behavioral effects almost exclusively among households with the financial flexibility to respond to price signals, while extracting progressively more from those without that flexibility, can credibly sustain a public-health justification — or whether it has become, in practice, a revenue instrument whose continued political viability rests on the public-health language that surrounds it rather than on the distributional equity that language implies.

The reconstructed receipt in Exhibit A provides one way of approaching that question directly. Selecting the lowest income quintile and reading the final line, then selecting the highest and observing how substantially that figure changes, provides an answer that five decades of policy documents have not offered: the receipt is the same, the store is the same, and the tax is the same — only the capacity to absorb it is not.
